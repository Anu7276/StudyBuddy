The Book That Saved the Earth Footprints without Feet — Prose

Chapter Summary

The Book That Saved the Earth is a humorous science fiction play written by Claire Boiko. The story is set in the year 2040 and imagines an attempted invasion of Earth by the people of Mars. The play is full of comedy and shows how misunderstanding, lack of knowledge, and taking things too literally can lead to funny and unexpected situations. It also highlights the importance of books, imagination, and intelligence.

The story begins in the twenty-fifth century, where a historian tells the audience about a famous event from the past known as "The Great Martian Invasion of 2040." According to history, the people of Earth were saved not by soldiers or powerful weapons, but by a simple old book called Mother Goose. The historian then begins explaining how this unbelievable event took place.

At that time, Mars was ruled by a powerful leader named Think-Tank. He was the commander of the Martian army and considered himself the most intelligent being in the universe. Think-Tank was proud, arrogant, and believed that the Martians were far more advanced than the people of Earth. He wanted to conquer Earth and make humans his servants.

To prepare for the invasion, Think-Tank sent one of his space crews to Earth. The team included Captain Omega, Lieutenant Iota, Sergeant Oop, and an apprentice named Noodle. Their mission was to collect information about Earth so that the Martians could easily defeat the humans.

After landing on Earth, the Martian crew entered a public library. Inside the library, they found many books placed on shelves. Since the Martians had never seen printed books before, they did not know what these strange objects were. Captain Omega informed Think-Tank that they had discovered hundreds of flat objects filled with black marks.

Think-Tank proudly tried to identify the objects. At first, he guessed that they were sandwiches because they looked flat. When his guess turned out to be wrong, he confidently announced that they were communication devices used by humans. Finally, after hearing more details, he correctly guessed that they were books. Even though he gave the correct answer in the end, it was only after making several funny and incorrect guesses.

Think-Tank ordered the Martians to read one of the books. However, the Martians had never learned to read English. Think-Tank then used his advanced machine to teach them the English language within a few seconds. The first book they picked up was a collection of nursery rhymes called Mother Goose.

The Martians started reading the nursery rhymes aloud. Unfortunately, they did not understand that nursery rhymes are imaginary and meant for children's entertainment. Instead, they took every word literally. This led to one misunderstanding after another.

When they read the rhyme "Mistress Mary, Quite Contrary", Think-Tank believed that Mary was one of the greatest military commanders on Earth because she was growing rows of "silver bells" and "cockle shells." He thought these were dangerous weapons being secretly prepared against Mars.

Next, they read the famous rhyme "Hey Diddle Diddle." In the rhyme, a cow jumps over the moon, a little dog laughs, and a dish runs away with a spoon. Think-Tank became completely confused. He believed that Earth animals possessed magical powers and that humans had trained them to perform impossible tasks. This made him even more afraid of Earth's strange abilities.

The greatest misunderstanding happened when the Martians read the rhyme about Humpty Dumpty. Think-Tank believed that Humpty Dumpty was the most powerful leader on Earth. Since Humpty Dumpty was actually an egg in the rhyme, Think-Tank concluded that the rulers of Earth were egg-shaped creatures. He became terrified after reading that "all the king's horses and all the king's men couldn't put Humpty Dumpty together again." He misunderstood this to mean that Earth possessed weapons so powerful that even their greatest leaders could be completely destroyed.

Think-Tank now became convinced that Earth was far more dangerous than Mars. He imagined that humans had powerful armies, magical animals, and deadly weapons beyond Martian understanding. Instead of attacking Earth, he immediately ordered his soldiers to cancel the invasion. He instructed everyone to return safely to Mars before the humans could attack them first.

After returning to Mars, Think-Tank became so frightened that he decided to move even farther away from Earth. Along with his followers, he settled on the planet Alpha Centauri, where he believed he would be safe from Earth's imaginary powers.

Meanwhile, there was never any real danger on Earth. The people continued living peacefully without even knowing that an invasion had almost taken place. A simple children's book filled with funny nursery rhymes had frightened the mighty Martian army and saved the entire planet without anyone realizing it.

The historian concludes the play by saying that this is why the old book Mother Goose is remembered as "The Book That Saved the Earth." The title is humorous because the book itself had no magical power. It saved Earth only because the Martians completely misunderstood its contents and believed ordinary nursery rhymes were descriptions of Earth's powerful weapons and strange customs.

Theme

The play highlights the importance of knowledge, imagination, books, and clear communication. It shows that misunderstanding facts and taking things literally can create unnecessary fear and confusion. The story also reminds us that intelligence without wisdom and common sense can lead to foolish decisions. Through humour, the play celebrates the value of literature and creative imagination.

Message for Students

This chapter teaches us that we should never make decisions without understanding the complete facts. Misunderstanding information can lead to fear, confusion, and wrong conclusions. Books increase our knowledge, improve our imagination, and help us understand different cultures and ideas. We should always think carefully, ask questions, and avoid judging situations without proper understanding. The play also reminds us that true intelligence comes with wisdom, humility, and the ability to understand things correctly.
