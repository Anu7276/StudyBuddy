CH 8

Memories of Childhood Vistas — Prose

Chapter Summary

Memories of Childhood is the last chapter of the Class 12 English textbook Vistas. It consists of two autobiographical accounts written by two famous women from different parts of the world—Zitkala-Sa, a Native American writer, and Bama, a well-known Tamil writer from India. Although they belong to different cultures and countries, both writers faced discrimination during their childhood. Their experiences highlight the importance of equality, self-respect, education, and courage in overcoming injustice.

Part I – The Cutting of My Long Hair

The first part is written by Zitkala-Sa, who belonged to a Native American tribe. When she was a young girl, she was sent to a boarding school run by white Americans. The purpose of these schools was to force Native American children to adopt the customs and culture of white society. The children were expected to forget their own traditions, language, and identity.

When Zitkala-Sa arrived at the school, everything seemed strange and unfamiliar. The school building was very large, and the rules were strict. The children had to follow fixed schedules for eating, studying, and sleeping. They were treated more like machines than individuals. Zitkala-Sa felt lonely and uncomfortable because she missed her home, family, and the freedom of her tribal life.

One day, she learned that all new students were required to have their long hair cut. In her Native American culture, long hair was a symbol of pride, courage, and identity. Only cowards or people who had been captured by enemies had their hair cut. Therefore, she believed that cutting her hair would be a great insult to her culture and traditions.

Determined to protect her identity, Zitkala-Sa decided to hide from the school authorities. She quietly climbed upstairs and hid under a bed in one of the rooms. However, after searching the entire building, the authorities found her. She cried, struggled, and resisted, but the adults held her firmly and cut off her long hair against her wishes.

This incident deeply hurt Zitkala-Sa. She felt powerless, humiliated, and heartbroken because she had been forced to give up an important part of her cultural identity. The experience remained in her memory throughout her life and became a symbol of the injustice suffered by Native American children under colonial rule.

Part II – We Too Are Human Beings

The second part is written by Bama, a famous Tamil writer from India. In this autobiographical account, she describes an incident from her childhood that made her aware of caste discrimination in Indian society.

As a young schoolgirl, Bama was returning home one day when she noticed an elderly man from her community carrying a packet of snacks. Instead of holding the packet with his hands, he carried it by its string, keeping it away from his body. Bama found this sight very amusing and laughed all the way home.

When she reached home, she excitedly narrated the incident to her elder brother, Annan. Instead of laughing, Annan became serious. He explained that the old man belonged to a lower caste and was carrying the packet that way because the upper-caste landlord believed that if a lower-caste person touched the food directly, it would become polluted or impure. Therefore, the old man had to carry it by the string as a mark of respect for the landlord.

Bama was shocked after hearing this explanation. She felt deeply hurt and angry that people were treated differently simply because of the caste into which they were born. She realized that innocent and hardworking people were being humiliated without any fault of their own. This experience made her understand the cruelty and unfairness of the caste system.

Seeing Bama's sadness, Annan advised her not to feel helpless. Instead, he encouraged her to study hard, gain knowledge, and become successful. He explained that education was the most powerful weapon against discrimination. If people from their community became educated and respected, society would eventually learn to treat them with equality and dignity.

Bama accepted her brother's advice with determination. She worked very hard in her studies and consistently performed well in school. Her academic success earned her respect from both teachers and classmates. Through education, she gained confidence and proved that a person's abilities are far more important than caste or social background.

Both Zitkala-Sa and Bama faced discrimination during their childhood, but neither accepted injustice silently. While Zitkala-Sa fought to protect her cultural identity, Bama chose education as a means of fighting social discrimination. Their experiences inspire readers to value equality, respect every individual, and stand against all forms of injustice.

Theme

The chapter highlights the themes of discrimination, cultural identity, social inequality, self-respect, education, courage, and human dignity. It strongly opposes racial and caste discrimination and emphasizes that every individual deserves equal respect regardless of culture, race, religion, or social background. The chapter also shows that education is one of the most powerful tools for bringing social change and achieving equality.

Message for Students

This chapter teaches us that every human being deserves equal respect and dignity. We should never discriminate against anyone based on caste, race, religion, language, or appearance. Like Zitkala-Sa and Bama, we should have the courage to stand against injustice and protect our identity and self-respect. The chapter also reminds us that education is the key to empowerment, confidence, and social progress. By treating everyone with kindness, fairness, and equality, we can build a better and more inclusive society.
