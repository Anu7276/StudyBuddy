Should Wizard Hit Mommy Vistas — Prose

Chapter Summary

Should Wizard Hit Mommy? is an interesting story written by John Updike. The chapter explores the relationship between parents and children, different ways of looking at the same situation, and the importance of love, discipline, and values. The story is presented as a bedtime story told by a father, Jack, to his four-year-old daughter, Jo. Through this story, the author compares the innocent thinking of children with the mature understanding of adults.

The story begins on a Saturday evening. Jack is putting his little daughter, Jo, to bed. Every evening, Jo asks her father to tell her a new story. Jack usually tells stories about a little animal named Roger, who faces some problem and then visits a wise wizard for help. Jo enjoys these stories very much because they always have interesting adventures and happy endings.

On this particular evening, Jack tells Jo the story of Roger Skunk. Roger Skunk is a small skunk who has one big problem. Like all skunks, he has a bad smell. Because of his unpleasant smell, none of the other animals wants to play with him. Whenever Roger tries to make friends, the animals run away from him. Roger feels lonely, sad, and rejected because everyone avoids him.

One day, Roger meets an old owl and tells him about his problem. The owl advises Roger to visit the Wizard, who is famous for solving difficult problems. Roger happily follows the owl's advice and reaches the Wizard's house deep inside the forest.

The Wizard welcomes Roger kindly and asks him about his problem. Roger explains that he smells bad and wants to smell like the other animals so that they will become his friends. The Wizard agrees to help him. In return, he asks Roger to pay seven pennies. Roger does not have enough money, so the Wizard magically helps him find the required seven pennies.

After receiving the payment, the Wizard waves his magic wand and changes Roger's smell from that of a skunk to the sweet fragrance of roses. Roger becomes extremely happy. As soon as he leaves the Wizard's house, all the other animals gather around him. They love his beautiful smell and happily invite him to play with them. For the first time in his life, Roger feels accepted and enjoys playing with his new friends.

Later that evening, Roger returns home feeling excited. However, his mother immediately notices that something is different. She cannot smell the usual skunk smell. When Roger proudly tells her about the Wizard's magic, she becomes angry. She believes that Roger should not change his natural identity just to please others. She wants her son to remain proud of who he is.

Roger's mother takes him back to the Wizard's house. She demands that the Wizard change Roger back to his original smell. The Wizard refuses because Roger had asked for the new smell and was happy with it. Roger's mother becomes angry and hits the Wizard on the head with her umbrella. Finally, the Wizard changes Roger's smell back to the natural skunk smell.

After returning home, Roger once again smells like every other skunk. Although the other animals may not want to play with him anymore, his mother is satisfied because she believes her son should accept his true identity instead of changing himself to gain others' approval.

At this point, Jo becomes upset with the ending. She believes that Roger should have been allowed to keep the pleasant smell because it made him happy and helped him make friends. She thinks Roger's mother was wrong to force him to change back. Jo repeatedly asks her father why the Wizard did not hit Roger's mother when she struck him with her umbrella.

Jack disagrees with Jo. He explains that Roger's mother was older, wiser, and knew what was best for her child. According to Jack, children should trust their parents because parents have more experience in life. He believes that Roger's mother wanted to teach her son an important lesson about accepting himself and not changing his identity just to please others.

Jo is not fully convinced by her father's explanation. She still feels that Roger deserved to be happy and that the Wizard should have defended himself. The conversation between Jack and Jo reveals the difference between the thinking of children and adults. While children often focus on immediate happiness and fairness, adults think about responsibility, discipline, values, and long-term benefits.

Throughout the story, Jack is also thinking about his own family life. His wife is expecting another child, and Jack feels that his responsibilities are increasing. This influences the way he tells the story and explains why he supports Roger's mother's decision. The story ends without giving a final answer, allowing readers to think about both viewpoints and decide which one they agree with.

Theme

The chapter explores the themes of parent-child relationships, love, discipline, identity, individuality, and different perspectives of children and adults. It highlights the conflict between a child's desire for happiness and an adult's concern for moral values and responsibility. The story also shows that people of different ages may understand the same situation in completely different ways.

Message for Students

This chapter teaches us that parents often make decisions because they believe those decisions are best for their children, even if children do not immediately understand them. At the same time, the story reminds parents to listen to their children's feelings and opinions with patience and understanding. True happiness comes from accepting ourselves, respecting our individuality, and maintaining open communication within the family. The chapter encourages both parents and children to understand each other's perspectives with love, trust, and mutual respect.
